    • The Solar System

    Introduction

    Our planet, the Earth, orbits around the Sun once every 365 days. There are many other bodies which are also bound by the Sun's gravitational pull. Together, these bodies form the Solar System.

    The Solar System contains one star, eight planets, 5 dwarf planets (officially), 293 moons (so far), thousands of comets and millions of asteroids. Our Sun orbits the centre of our galaxy - the Milky Way, taking 230 million years to complete one orbit.

    The Planets

    There are eight planets in the Solar System: Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus and Neptune. The inner four planets are rocky and small, known as the terrestrial planets. Separating Mars and Jupiter is the asteroid belt, a ring of rock and ice. Sometimes asteroids are sent hurtling towards Earth - known as Near-Earth Asteroids (NEAs). Jupiter and Saturn are the gas giants, which are far more massive than the inner rocky planets. Uranus and Neptune are the outermost planets and are known as the ice giants.

    The word "planet" comes from the ancient Greek plan̅et, meaning wanderer. This refers to the fact that ancient astronomers observed that some stars would move across the night sky, changing their positions relative to the "fixed" stars. A modern definition comes from the International Astronomical Union (IAU). This definition states that a planet must:

    1. Orbit a star (such as the Sun)
    2. Be big enough that its gravity is able to force it into a spherical shape
    3. Be big enough that it is capable of clearing away any other objects in its orbit of a similar size
    Note that in this definition Pluto is not a planet, but instead it is a dwarf planet.
